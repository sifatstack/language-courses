✅ Week 1: Greetings, Introductions, and Social Basics ▶

1.1 -- Greetings

Syria is a culturally diverse country. The most universal greeting is the Islamic greeting, used by Muslims and widely understood by all Syrians:

اَلسَّلَامُ عَلَيْكُم (as-salamu alaykum) -- Peace be upon you

وَعَلَيْكُمُ ٱلسَّلَام (wa-alaykum as-salam) -- And upon you peace (response)

Other greetings you'll hear constantly:

مَرْحَبَا (marhaba) -- Hello (universal, any context)

أَهْلًا وَسَهْلًا (ahlan wa-sahlan) -- Welcome (very warm, used as greeting too)

أَهْلِين (ahlen) -- Casual shortened "ahlan" -- very common

كِيفَك؟ / كِيفِك؟ (kifak? m / kifik? f) -- How are you? (THE most common daily greeting)

شْلُونَك؟ / شْلُونِك؟ (shlonak? m / shlonik? f) -- How are you? (also very common)

شُو أَخْبَارَك؟ / أَخْبَارِك؟ (shu akhbarak? m / akhbarik? f) -- What's your news?

Responses:

اَلْحَمْدُ لِلَّه، مْنِيح / مْنِيحَة (il-hamdu lillah, mnih / mniha) -- Thank God, good

تَمَام (tamam) -- Perfect / Great

مَاشِي ٱلْحَال (mashi l-hal) -- Getting by / So-so

هِيك هِيك (hek hek) -- So-so

Saying goodbye:

ٱللَّه مَعَك / مَعِك (alla ma'ak / ma'ik) -- God be with you (most common goodbye)

مَعَ ٱلسَّلَامِة (ma' is-salame) -- Goodbye

يَلَّا بَاي (yalla bay) -- Alright, bye! (casual)

بْشُوفَك / بْشُوفِك (bshufak / bshufik) -- See you

1.2 -- The Gender Distinction (Masculine / Feminine)

This is one of the most important features of Arabic. Unlike Spanish's tu/usted, Syrian Arabic doesn't have formal "you" -- but it ALWAYS distinguishes gender.

AddressingArabic "you"Example: "How are you?"
One maleإِنْتَ (inte)كِيفَك؟ (kifak?)
One femaleإِنْتِ (inti)كِيفِك؟ (kifik?)
Group (plural)إِنْتُو (intu)كِيفْكُن؟ (kifkun?)
Cultural note: Syrian Arabic doesn't have a formal "you" like French vous or Spanish usted. Respect is shown through tone, titles, and expressions. Some speakers use plural إِنْتُو for a single person as respect, especially with elders. Use titles like عَمُّو (ammu / uncle) or خَالْتُو (khaltu / auntie) for older people.

1.5 -- Dialogue Practice

Read this dialogue aloud. Pay attention to the gender markers:

Ahmad: مَرْحَبَا! كِيفِك؟

(marhaba! kifik?) "Hello! How are you?" (to a female)

Sara: أَهْلِين! اَلْحَمْدُ لِلَّه، مْنِيحَة. وِإِنْتَ كِيفَك؟

(ahlen! il-hamdu lillah, mniha. w-inte kifak?) "Hi! Thank God, good. And you?"

Ahmad: تَمَام، اَلْحَمْدُ لِلَّه. شُو ٱسْمِك؟

(tamam, il-hamdu lillah. shu ismik?) "Great, thank God. What's your name?"

Sara: ٱسْمِي سَارَة. وِإِنْتَ؟

(ismi Sara. w-inte?) "My name is Sara. And you?"

Ahmad: أَنَا أَحْمَد. مِنْ وِين إِنْتِ؟

(ana Ahmad. min wen inti?) "I'm Ahmad. Where are you from?"

Sara: أَنَا مِنْ أَمْرِيكَا، بَسْ عَمْ بِتْعَلَّم عَرَبِي.

(ana min Amrika, bass am bit'allam arabi.) "I'm from America, but I'm learning Arabic."

Ahmad: مَاشَاءَ ٱللَّه! تْشَرَّفْنَا يَا سَارَة.

(masha'alla! tsharrafna ya Sara.) "Wonderful! Pleased to meet you, Sara."

Sara: أَهْلًا فِيك. ٱللَّه مَعَك!

(ahlan fik. alla ma'ak!) "Nice to meet you too. Goodbye!"

Note: Ahmad uses feminine forms (كِيفِك، ٱسْمِك) because he's speaking to Sara (female). Sara uses masculine forms (كِيفَك، فِيك، مَعَك) because she's speaking to Ahmad (male). The word يَا (ya) before a name is like "hey" -- used constantly and affectionately.

✅ Week 2: Essential Needs -- Asking, Wanting, Having ▶

2.1 -- "I want / I need" -- بِدِّي (biddi)

The word بِدِّي (biddi) is THE Syrian way to say "I want." This is one of the biggest markers of Levantine Arabic.

PersonArabicPronunciationMeaning
IبِدِّيbiddiI want
You (m)بِدَّكbiddakyou want
You (f)بِدِّكbiddikyou want
Heبِدُّوbidduhe wants
Sheبِدَّاbiddashe wants
Weبِدْنَاbiddnawe want
Theyبِدْهُنbiddhunthey want

Examples:

بِدِّي مَيّ (biddi mayy) -- I want water

بِدِّي قَهْوِة (biddi ahwe) -- I want coffee

بِدِّي تَكْسِي (biddi taksi) -- I want a taxi

بِدِّي مُسَاعَدِة (biddi musa'ade) -- I need help

Negative -- "I don't want":

مَا بِدِّي (ma biddi) -- I don't want

مَا بِدِّي شِي (ma biddi shi) -- I don't want anything

2.2 -- "There is / There isn't" -- فِي / مَا فِي

فِي مَيّ؟ (fi mayy?) -- Is there water?

إِي، فِي (e, fi) -- Yes, there is

لَأ، مَا فِي (la', ma fi) -- No, there isn't

وِين فِي حَمَّام؟ (wen fi hammam?) -- Where is there a bathroom?

مَا فِي مُشْكْلِة (ma fi mushkle) -- No problem

Levantine marker: فِي (fi) for "there is" is distinctly Levantine/colloquial. MSA would say يُوجَد (yujad). If you say فِي, people know you're speaking colloquial.

2.5 -- Dialogue: At a Coffee Shop

You: مَرْحَبَا! بِدِّي قَهْوِة، لَوْ سَمَحْت.

(marhaba! biddi ahwe, law samaht.) "Hello! I'd like a coffee, please."

Barista: أَهْلِين! سَادِة وِلَّا بْسُكَّر؟

(ahlen! sade willa b-sukkar?) "Hi! Plain or with sugar?"

You: وَسَط، لَوْ سَمَحْت.

(wasat, law samaht.) "Medium (sugar), please."

Barista: بِدَّك شِي تَانِي؟

(biddak shi tani?) "Do you want anything else?"

(tamam. tfaddal.) "Done. Here you go."

You: قَدِّيش ٱلْحِسَاب؟

(addesh il-hsab?) "How much is the bill?"

You: تْفَضَّل. يِسْلَمُو!

(tfaddal. yislamu!) "Here you go. Thanks!"

3.4 -- Dialogue: Taking a Taxi

You're near بَابِ تُومَا (Bab Tuma), the famous Christian quarter gate in Old Damascus, heading to اَلْمَزِّة (al-Mazze).

You: مَرْحَبَا! عَلَى ٱلْمَزِّة، قَدِّيش؟

(marhaba! 'ala l-mazze, addesh?) "Hello! To Mazzeh, how much?"

Driver: أَهْلِين! ٱلْمَزِّة؟ عِشْرِين أَلْف.

(ahlen! il-mazze? 'ishrin alf.) "Hi! Mazzeh? Twenty thousand."

You: كْتِير غَالِي يَا أَخِي! عَشَرَة مَاشِي؟

(ktir ghali ya akhi! 'ashara mashi?) "That's too much, brother! Ten thousand okay?"

Driver: لَأ وَٱللَّه، بَعِيدِة. خَمَسْطَعْش آخِر شِي.

(la' walla, ba'ide. khamasta'sh akhir shi.) "No, I swear, it's far. Fifteen is my last offer."

You: طَيِّب، خَمَسْطَعْش مَاشِي. يَلَّا.

(tayyib, khamasta'sh mashi. yalla.) "Okay, fifteen works. Let's go."

[Arriving...]

You: نَزِّلْنِي هُون، لَوْ سَمَحْت.

(nazzilni hon, law samaht.) "Drop me off here, please."

You: تْفَضَّل، خَمَسْطَعْش. ٱللَّه يَعْطِيك ٱلْعَافْيِة!

(tfaddal, khamasta'sh. alla ya'tik il-'afye!) "Here's fifteen. God give you strength!"

Driver: ٱللَّه يْعَافِيك! مَعَ ٱلسَّلَامِة.

(alla y'afik! ma'a s-salame.) "God keep you well! Goodbye."

Key vocabulary:

سَوَّاق (sawwa') -- driver

يَا أَخِي (ya akhi) -- brother (used casually, like "bro")

نَزِّلْنِي (nazzilni) -- drop me off

هَلَّق (halla') -- now (distinctly Syrian)

Taxi tips: Always agree on the price BEFORE getting in. Say your destination and ask قَدِّيش؟ immediately. نَزِّلْنِي هُون (nazzilni hon) "drop me off here" is essential in Damascus traffic.
