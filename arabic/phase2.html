4.1 -- How Syrian Arabic Verbs Work

Arabic verbs are built on a root system -- usually three consonants that carry a core meaning. By changing the vowels and adding prefixes or suffixes, you create different words all related to the same concept.

Take the root k-t-b (ك-ت-ب), which carries the core meaning of "writing":

WordPronunciationMeaningPattern
كَتَبَkatabhe wrotepast tense verb
بْيِكْتُبbyiktubhe writespresent tense verb
كِتَابktaba booknoun (object)
مَكْتَبmaktaban office / deskplace noun
مَكْتَبِةmaktabea library / bookshopplace noun (feminine)

All five words revolve around the same three letters: ك-ت-ب. Once you recognize a root, you can often guess the meaning of new words built from it.

Key difference from MSA: In Syrian Arabic, the present tense always takes a بـ (b-) prefix. MSA says يَكْتُبُ (yaktubu), but Syrians say بْيِكْتُب (byiktub). This بـ prefix is the single most important marker of colloquial present tense.

4.2 -- Present Simple Conjugation

Let's conjugate كَتَبَ (katab) -- "to write" -- in the present tense:

PersonArabicPronunciationMeaning
Iأَنَا بِكْتُبana biktubI write
You (m)إِنْتَ بْتِكْتُبinte btiktubyou write
You (f)إِنْتِ بْتِكْتْبِيinti btiktbiyou write
Heهُوِّ بْيِكْتُبhuwwe byiktubhe writes
Sheهِيِّ بْتِكْتُبhiyye btiktubshe writes
Weنِحْنَا مْنِكْتُبnihna mniktubwe write
You (pl)إِنْتُو بْتِكْتْبُوintu btiktbuyou all write
Theyهِنِّ بْيِكْتْبُوhinne byiktbuthey write

The prefix pattern:

I → بـ (b-)

You (m) / She → بْتـ (bt-)

You (f) → بْتـ (bt-) + ـي (-i) suffix

He → بْيـ (by-)

We → مْنـ (mn-)

Plural (you all / they) → same prefix + ـو (-u) suffix

Memory trick: "You (m)" and "She" share the exact same verb form (بْتِكْتُب). Context always makes it clear. The "we" form is the odd one out with its مْنـ (mn-) prefix -- think of نِحْنَا (nihna) starting with "n".

4.3 -- Present Continuous: "I am doing right now"

To say you're doing something right now (like Spanish estoy comiendo), add عَمْ ('am) before the present tense verb:

عَمْ بَاكُل ('am bakul) -- I am eating (right now)

عَمْ بِشْرَب شَاي ('am bishrab shay) -- I am drinking tea

عَمْ بَحْكِي عَ ٱلتِّلِفُون ('am bahki 'a it-tilifon) -- I'm talking on the phone

عَمْ بْيِكْتُب رِسَالِة ('am byiktub risale) -- He is writing a letter

عَمْ بْتِطْبُخ ('am btitbukh) -- She is cooking

عَمْ مْنِدْرُس ('am mnidrus) -- We are studying

Simple vs. continuous: بَاكُل (bakul) = "I eat" (habitual). عَمْ بَاكُل ('am bakul) = "I am eating" (right now). Like Spanish como vs. estoy comiendo.

Asking "What are you doing?":

شُو عَمْ تَعْمِل؟ / تَعْمْلِي؟ (shu 'am ta'mil? m / ta'mli? f) -- What are you doing?

شُو عَمْ يِعْمِل؟ (shu 'am yi'mil?) -- What is he doing?

وِين عَمْ تْرُوح؟ / تْرُوحِي؟ (wen 'am truh? m / truhi? f) -- Where are you going?

Note: After عَمْ, you may sometimes hear the verb without the بـ prefix. Both forms are used in Syria.

4.4 -- Can / Can't: بَقْدَر / مَا بَقْدَر

To say "I can" or "I can't," use بَقْدَر (ba'dar) / مَا بَقْدَر (ma ba'dar). The verb that follows drops its بـ prefix:

بَقْدَر أَحْكِي عَرَبِي (ba'dar ahki arabi) -- I can speak Arabic

مَا بَقْدَر أَنَام (ma ba'dar anam) -- I can't sleep

بَقْدَر أَسَاعْدَك؟ (ba'dar asa'dak?) -- Can I help you?

بْتِقْدَر تْسَاعِدْنِي؟ (bti'dar tsa'idni?) -- Can you (m) help me?

بْتِقْدْرِي تْحْكِي أَبْطَا؟ (bti'dri thki abta?) -- Can you (f) speak slower?

Important: After بَقْدَر, the following verb loses its بـ prefix. You say بَقْدَر أَحْكِي, NOT بَقْدَر بَحْكِي. Like Spanish puedo hablar -- the second verb goes to infinitive form.

Conjugation of بَقْدَر (can):

You (f)بْتِقْدْرِيbti'dri
Heبْيِقْدَرbyi'dar
Sheبْتِقْدَرbti'dar
Weمْنِقْدَرmni'dar

Useful phrases:

بْتِقْدَر تْعِيد؟ (bti'dar t'id?) -- Can you repeat (that)?

مَا بَقْدَر أَجِي بُكْرَا (ma ba'dar aji bukra) -- I can't come tomorrow

بَقْدَر أَدْفَع بِالْكَارْت؟ (ba'dar adfa' bil-kart?) -- Can I pay by card?

5.1 -- Simple Past ("I did")

Good news: the past tense in Syrian Arabic is simpler than the present. No بـ prefix -- just the root with suffixes.

Let's conjugate كَتَبَ (katab / wrote):

PersonArabicPronunciationMeaning
IكَتَبْتkatabtI wrote
You (m)كَتَبْتkatabtyou wrote
You (f)كَتَبْتِيkatabtiyou wrote
Heكَتَبَkatabhe wrote
Sheكَتْبِتkatbitshe wrote
Weكَتَبْنَاkatabnawe wrote
You (pl)كَتَبْتُوkatabtuyou all wrote
Theyكَتَبُوkatabuthey wrote

The pattern (suffixes):

I / You (m) → ـت (-t)

You (f) → ـتِي (-ti)

He → (base form, no suffix)

She → ـِت (-it)

We → ـنَا (-na)

You (pl) → ـتُو (-tu)

They → ـو (-u)

5.2 -- Talking About the Recent Past

Syrian Arabic doesn't have a separate "present perfect" like English. The simple past covers both "I did" and "I have done." Context makes it clear:

أَكَلْت (akalt) -- I ate / I have eaten

خَلَّصْت ٱلشُّغُل (khallast ish-shughul) -- I finished / I've finished the work

لِسَّا مَا أَكَلْت (lissa ma akalt) -- I haven't eaten yet (لِسَّا = "still/yet" -- very useful!)

لِسَّا (lissa) = "still/yet" -- very useful for expressing "haven't done yet."

7.2 -- Yes/No Questions

In Syrian Arabic, you make a yes/no question simply by changing your intonation (raising the pitch at the end). No special grammar needed!

إِنْتَ سُورِي. (inte suri.) -- You are Syrian. (statement)

إِنْتَ سُورِي؟ (inte suri?) -- Are you Syrian? (question -- just raise pitch)

بْتْحِبّ ٱلْقَهْوِة؟ (bthibb il-ahwe?) -- Do you like coffee?

أَكَلْت؟ (akalt?) -- Did you eat? / Have you eaten?

هَالْقَدّ (ha-l-add) -- This much / So much

مَرَّة (marra) -- Really / Very (casual emphasis)

وَٱللَّه (walla) -- Really / I swear (constant everyday emphasis, not just literal)
