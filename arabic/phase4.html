Question 1 of 3
What does the proverb ٱللِّي مَا بْيِعْرِف ٱلصَّقِر بْيِشْوِيه mean?
Literally "He who doesn't know the falcon, grills it" -- meaning if you don't recognize something's worth, you'll waste it.
Question 2 of 3
Which proverb means "Live within your means"?
"Stretch your legs to the length of your blanket" -- don't overextend yourself financially.
Question 3 of 3
What is the Arabic word for "proverbs"?
أَمْثَال (amthal) means "proverbs" or "sayings." Syrians use them constantly in conversation.

15.2 -- Spanish-Arabic Connection

Spanish and Arabic share over 4,000 words thanks to 800 years of Moorish rule in Spain (711--1492). If you speak Spanish, you already know more Arabic than you think!

The al- connection: Notice all those Spanish words starting with al- (aldea, alfombra, algodon, alcalde, algebra)? That's the Arabic definite article ٱلـ (il- / al-) baked right into the Spanish word! Even Spanish place names: Alhambra = ٱلْحَمْرَا (il-hamra, "the red one"), Guadalquivir = ٱلْوَادِي ٱلْكْبِير (il-wadi l-kbir, "the great valley").

Question 1 of 3
The Spanish word "ojala" comes from which Arabic phrase?
Spanish "ojala" (meaning "I hope so") comes directly from إِنْ شَاءَ ٱللَّه (insha'alla) -- "God willing."
Question 2 of 3
Why do so many Spanish words start with "al-" (aldea, alfombra, algebra)?
The Arabic definite article ٱلـ (il- / al-) was absorbed into Spanish during 800 years of Moorish rule, becoming permanently fused to the word.
Question 3 of 3
What is the Arabic word for "lemon" that became Spanish "limon"?
لِيمُون (laymun) became Spanish "limon." نَارِنْج (naranj) became "naranja" (orange).

15.3 -- Grammar Quick Reference

Negation Summary

TenseHow to negateExample
Present مَا + verb (keep بـ) مَا بْعَرِف (ma baʿrif) -- I don't know
Past مَا + verb مَا رُحْت (ma ruht) -- I didn't go
Future مَا رَح + verb مَا رَح أَجِي (ma rah aji) -- I won't come
Noun/adjective مُو / مِش مُو مْنِيح (mu mnih) -- Not good
There isn't مَا فِي مَا فِي مَحَلّ (ma fi mahall) -- There's no shop
Don't (command) لَا + verb لَا تْرُوح! (la truh!) -- Don't go!

Question 1 of 3
How do you say "I didn't go" in Syrian Arabic?
Past tense is negated with مَا + verb. مُو/مِش are used for nouns and adjectives, not verbs.
Question 2 of 3
What is the possessive suffix for "her" in Syrian Arabic?
ـهَا (-ha) is the suffix for "her" -- both object and possessive. For example: بِيتْهَا (betha) = her house.
Question 3 of 3
How do you form the present continuous in Syrian Arabic?
عَمْ (ʿam) + بـ (b-) + verb creates the present continuous. Example: عَمْ بْرُوح = "I am going."

15.4 -- Practice Strategy

Daily Routine (15--20 minutes)

5 minutes -- Review: Re-read your latest week's vocabulary and phrases out loud

10 minutes -- Practice with Claude: Use the practice prompt from your current week

5 minutes -- Listen: Watch a short Syrian Arabic clip (YouTube: Syrian drama scenes, cooking videos, or street interviews)

Weekly Goals

Weeks 1--3: Survive. Greet people, ask basic questions, count, get around.

Weeks 4--8: Build. Use all three tenses, describe things, express opinions.

Weeks 9--14: Connect. Have real conversations about food, family, feelings, and daily life.

Ongoing: Absorb culture. Learn proverbs, understand humor, master the reactions.

Recommended Resources

YouTube channels: Search for "Syrian Arabic lessons" or تَعَلَّم ٱللَّهْجَة ٱلشَّامِيَّة

TV shows: Syrian dramas (مُسَلْسَلَات سُورِيِّة) -- start with comedies like بُقْعَة ضَوْء (Bu'ʿit Ḍaw')

Music: Syrian artists like George Wassouf, Assala Nasri, Sabah Fakhri (classical)

Practice partner: Find a Syrian conversation partner -- Syrians are incredibly warm and encouraging to anyone learning their language

Your Master Claude Prompt

Copy this prompt to practice everything: "You are my Syrian Levantine Arabic tutor. I'm a beginner learning Damascus dialect. Please:
1. Always write Arabic in Arabic script with full diacritics (tashkeel)
2. Include pronunciation in Latin letters after each Arabic phrase
3. Start each session by asking كِيفَك ٱلْيَوْم؟ and have a short warm-up conversation
4. Focus on the topic I request, using vocabulary and grammar appropriate to my level
5. Gently correct my mistakes by showing the correct form
6. Point out connections to Spanish when relevant
7. Teach me a new cultural insight or proverb each session
8. Keep responses conversational -- I want to feel like I'm chatting with a Syrian friend!"
