How to use this guide: This guide teaches spoken Bangladeshi Bangla -- the language people actually use in conversation. Every example shows: বাংলা (Bangla script) → Pronunciation → Meaning. Since you already know Syrian Arabic, you have a secret advantage -- Bangla has hundreds of Arabic loanwords!
Script note: Unlike Arabic, Bangla script runs left-to-right. There are no dots, no connected letters, and vowels have their own full forms AND shortened forms that attach to consonants. The biggest adjustment will be training your eyes to read left-to-right!
Bangladeshi vs. West Bengali: We teach Bangladeshi standard colloquial -- পানি (pani) not জল (jol), নাই (nai) not নি (ni). Think of it like how your Damascene Arabic is understood everywhere, while regional dialects have their own flavor.

✅ Week 1: Greetings, Introductions, and Social Basics ▶

1.1 -- Greetings

The most common greeting in Bangladesh is one you already know perfectly:

আসসালামু আলাইকুম (Assalamu Alaikum) -- Peace be upon you

ওয়ালাইকুম আসসালাম (Walaikum Assalam) -- And upon you peace

From your Arabic: This is exactly السلام عليكم / وعليكم السلام! Even many non-Muslims in Bangladesh use it casually. The pronunciation is nearly identical to Syrian Arabic.

Other greetings you'll hear:

নমস্কার (Nomoshkar) -- Hello (Hindu/secular formal greeting)

স্লামালকুম (Slamalekum) -- Casual, shortened version -- very common

কী খবর? (Ki khobor?) -- What's the news? / What's up?

কেমন আছো? (Kemon achho?) -- How are you? (informal)

কেমন আছেন? (Kemon achhen?) -- How are you? (formal/respectful)

Arabic connection: খবর (khobor) is from خبر (khabar) -- "news." So কী খবর? is literally شو الخبر؟ -- same question you'd ask in Damascus!

Responses:

ভালো আছি (Bhalo achhi) -- I'm well

আলহামদুলিল্লাহ, ভালো (Alhamdulillah, bhalo) -- Fine, praise God (you know this! الحمد لله)

এই চলতেছে (Ei choltechhe) -- Just getting by (like ماشي الحال)

মোটামুটি (Motamuti) -- So-so / More or less (like هيك هيك)

Saying goodbye:

আল্লাহ হাফেজ (Allah Hafez) -- God protect you / Goodbye (most common -- from الله حافظك!)

খোদা হাফেজ (Khoda Hafez) -- Same meaning, Persian-origin version, also used

আসি তাহলে (Ashi tahole) -- I'll go then / I'll get going

দেখা হবে (Dekha hobe) -- We'll see each other (like بشوفك)

আবার দেখা হবে (Abar dekha hobe) -- We'll meet again

1.2 -- The তুমি / আপনি / তুই Distinction

This is one of the most important social concepts in Bangla. Think of it as the tu/usted system from Spanish, but with three levels. In Syrian Arabic, you show respect through tone and titles (عمّو, خالتو) -- Bangla does it through the actual pronoun and verb conjugation.

LevelBanglaLike...When to use
Very intimateতুই (tui)Even more casual than tuVery close childhood friends, children, younger siblings. Can be rude if misused!
Informalতুমি (tumi)tu in SpanishFriends, peers, younger relatives, spouse. Your default "you."
Formalআপনি (apni)usted in SpanishElders, strangers, authority figures, in-laws. When in doubt, use this.

Every verb conjugates differently depending on which form you use:

Level"How are you?"Pronunciation
তুইকেমন আছিস?Kemon achhis?
তুমিকেমন আছো?Kemon achho?
আপনিকেমন আছেন?Kemon achhen?
Cultural note: In Bangladesh, always err on the side of আপনি (apni). Using তুমি with someone who expects আপনি is a social mistake. Using তুই with the wrong person can be genuinely offensive. This is like accidentally calling someone's father إنتَ instead of عمّو.

Question 1 of 3
You are meeting your friend's father for the first time. Which pronoun should you use?
আপনি (apni) is the formal/respectful level. Always use this with elders and strangers.
Question 2 of 3
How do you say "How are you?" to a friend using তুমি?

Question 3 of 3
How does Bangla differ from Syrian Arabic in showing respect?
Syrian Arabic shows respect through tone and titles (عمّو، خالتو). Bangla builds respect into the pronoun choice (তুই/তুমি/আপনি) and verb conjugation.

✅ Week 2: Essential Needs -- Asking, Wanting, Having ▶

2.1 -- "I want / I need" -- চাই (chai)

The verb চাওয়া (chaowa) means "to want." The most useful form is:

আমি ... চাই (Ami ... chai) -- I want ...

Syrian parallel: This works like بدّي (biddi)! Just put what you want after আমি and before চাই. In Syrian you say بدّي مي, in Bangla you say আমি পানি চাই -- "I want water."

আমি পানি চাই (Ami pani chai) -- I want water

আমি ভাত চাই (Ami bhat chai) -- I want rice

আমি চা চাই (Ami cha chai) -- I want tea

আমি একটা রিকশা চাই (Ami ekta rickshaw chai) -- I want a rickshaw

আমি সাহায্য চাই (Ami shahajjo chai) -- I need help

Negative -- "I don't want":

আমি চাই না (Ami chai na) -- I don't want

আমি ভাত চাই না (Ami bhat chai na) -- I don't want rice

আমাকে আর লাগবে না (Amake ar lagbe na) -- I don't need any more (like خلص، ما بدّي كمان)

Asking others:

আপনি কী চান? (Apni ki chan?) -- What do you want? (formal)

তুমি কী চাও? (Tumi ki chao?) -- What do you want? (informal)

2.2 -- "There is / There isn't" -- আছে / নাই

আছে (achhe) = there is / exists. নাই (nai) = there isn't / doesn't exist.

Syrian parallel: This pair works exactly like في / ما في (fi / ma fi)! পানি আছে? = في مي؟ and না, নাই = لأ، ما في.

পানি আছে? (Pani achhe?) -- Is there water?

হ্যাঁ, আছে (Hae, achhe) -- Yes, there is

না, নাই (Na, nai) -- No, there isn't

বাথরুম কোথায় আছে? (Bathroom kothay achhe?) -- Where is the bathroom?

ডাক্তার আছে? (Daktar achhe?) -- Is there a doctor?

সমস্যা নাই (Shomoshsha nai) -- No problem (ما في مشكلة)

টাকা নাই (Taka nai) -- No money (ما في مصاري)

BD marker: নাই (nai) instead of নেই (nei) is distinctly Bangladeshi. You'll hear it everywhere. This is like how هلّق marks you as Syrian vs. Egyptian دلوقتي.

2.5 -- Dialogue: At a Tea Stall (চায়ের দোকান)

Tea stalls are the social heart of Bangladesh -- like the قهوة (coffee shop) in Syria.

আপনি: ভাই, এক কাপ চা দেন।
(Apni: Bhai, ek kap cha den.)
"Brother, give me one cup of tea."

দোকানদার: চিনি দিবো?
(Dokandar: Chini dibo?)
"Should I add sugar?"

আপনি: হ্যাঁ, একটু কম দিবেন।
(Apni: Hae, ektu kom diben.)
"Yes, a little less please."

দোকানদার: আর কিছু লাগবে?
(Dokandar: Ar kichhu lagbe?)
"Need anything else?" (like بدّك شي تاني؟)

আপনি: একটা সিঙারা দেন।
(Apni: Ekta shingara den.)
"Give me one singara (samosa)."

দোকানদার: ঠিক আছে। পনেরো টাকা।
(Dokandar: Thik achhe. Ponero taka.)
"Okay. Fifteen taka."

(Rickshawala: Jabo. Koto diben?)
"I'll go. How much will you pay?"

আপনি: পঞ্চাশ টাকা।
(Apni: Ponchash taka.)
"Fifty taka."

(Rickshawala: Na bhai, eksho lagbe. Onek dur.)
"No brother, it'll be a hundred. Very far."
